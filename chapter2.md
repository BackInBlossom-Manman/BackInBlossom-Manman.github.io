### 2.1 Introduction
This chapter aims to critically review the current literature regarding the increase in extreme weather events' intensity and frequency impact on agricultural production and the transition from the physical world to the economic system and the financial market under a climate change background, which will lead to risk for global food security and systemic financial risk and finally cause both macro and micro level vulnerability. This literature review aims to identify key research gaps in previous studies and provide directions and a theoretical foundation for the subsequent model-building and data analysis in this study. 

### 2.2 Agricultural Vulnerability and Global Food Security
A primary manifestation of long-term climate change is the enhancement of extreme weather events’ intensity, frequency and duration, which already has a direct impact on the agricultural production (Lobell et al., 2011; Iizumi & Ramankutty, 2016) and cause risk for food security (Battisti &  Naylor, 2009), which not only impact on availability but also influence access, utilization and stability (Wheeler & von Braun, 2013). The negative impact is also evidenced by another study conducted by Ray et al. (2019), which found that around half of food-insecure countries experienced a decrease in the availability of consumable food calories. In the context of global climate change, it can account for approximately one-third of the yield change (Ray et al., 2015). The negative impact on main crops due to climate change has been calculated, with yield decreases of 3.8% and 5.5% for maize and wheat, respectively (Lobell et al., 2011).
Another study supports this argument, showing that wheat production decreased due to an increase in extreme events (Zampieri et al., 2017). Although studies have shown that some adoption strategies can mitigate climate risk for agriculture, for example, Kukal and Irmak (2018) argue that irrigated crops are less sensitive to climate change compared to non-irrigated crops, which suggests that irrigation is an effective method for mitigating climate risk. However, further research shows that the systematic resilience framework should be built against the increasing trend of extreme weather events (like drought, heatwave and flood), which threaten the sustainability of agricultural production, leading to food and nutritional insecurity and enhancing fluctuations in the price of agricultural production (Extreme weather and resilience of the global food system, 2015)
In summary, this research demonstrates that under a long-term climate change backdrop, the increase in short-term extreme weather events already weakens agricultural resilience and poses a long-term risk to sustainable agricultural production, which provides a background for the following chapter's further analysis of the physical impact of extreme weather events on agriculture. 

### 2.3 The Physical Impacts of Extreme Weather Events on Agriculture
This chapter will discuss the impact of extreme temperatures, precipitation, and compound events on agricultural yields, with an understanding of the principles behind crops' responses to different weather conditions. 

#### 2.3.1 The Effects of High Temperature and Heatwaves

**Non-linear relationships and threshold effect**
The key point of this finding is that an optimal temperature (29°C-30°C) exists for the crops' growth. Before this point, the increase in temperature improves yield, but after this limit, further increases in temperature harm yield, resulting in a non-linear relationship. Schlenker and Roberts (2009) first conducted a panel data regression model analysis of the USA county-level crop yields, finding that the threshold effect exists for the relationship of temperature, the yield of maize, soybean and cotton will increase with temperature before reaching the temperature threshold  (maize: 29°C, soybean: 30°C, cotton: 32°C) then decline rapidly after this point, which also shows an non-linear relationship between yield and temperature.
Later after Schauberger et al., (2017) conduct the same panel regression method on yield historical dataset 1980-2010 reached similar conclusions, the negative response of rain-fed corn and soybeans to high temperature (over 30°C), but they further revealed a regional difference under the same conditions the same crops in irrigated regions are less sensitive to same high temperature, this evidenced the assumption of water stress caused by heatwave is the main driver for yield decrease. Siebert et al. (2017) further reveal another reason for the mitigation effect of irrigation: irrigation has a cooling effect through evapotranspiration.

**Impact mechanism: Phenology and Physiology**
Studies on the effects of climate conditions on crop physiological mechanisms reveal that these mechanisms adapt as a natural response to extreme weather conditions, which can compromise the crop's growth and yield. Tao et al. (2006) investigated six representative sites to determine whether weather conditions impact the key growth stage of crops. They revealed that under global warming scenarios, the change in phenological stage leads to a mismatch between the key growth stage and optimal weather conditions, resulting in a reduction in yield levels. A later study from a biological perspective reveals the mechanism by which high temperatures cause physiological damage to crops (Hatfield & Prueger, 2015).

**Varietal Differences and Regional Differences**
The crops' response to similar weather conditions varies in different regions with specific climate patterns. The previous differences in irrigation conditions demonstrate the regional differences between rain-fed and irrigated crops under high-temperature conditions (Kukal & Irmak, 2018).
The research on phenological stage change (Tao et al., 2006) also reveals regional differences in the temperature rise's effect on crop yield, with yield reduction in North and Northwest China, as well as in Central China, and a yield increase in Northeast China, all of which exist simultaneously. Butler and Huybers (2013) illustrate a spatial adaptation of maize in climatologically hotter counties; the yield loss and yield enhancement in cool northwestern regions are larger in response to killing degree days (KDDs), and the northeast area is most sensitive to growing degree days (GDDs). 
The different crops also exhibit varying responses to climate change; the net change in yield for rice and soybeans is less significant compared to maize under global warming (Lobell et al., 2011).
Moreover, the same crops’ different varieties also exhibit sensitivity variance under similar weather conditions; heat-tolerant maize cultivars from southern regions are less sensitive to temperature increases (Butler & Huybers, 2013); new high-yield wheat cultivars, however, show more sensitivity to high temperatures, indicating a trade-off between yield and heat tolerance (Tack et al., 2015).

#### 2.3.2 The Effects of Precipitation Changes, Droughts, and Water Excess
Global warming has also led to changes in precipitation patterns, resulting in more frequent and extreme precipitation events, such as droughts and excess precipitation, which negatively impact crop yields.

**Threat of Drought**
In line with the trend of global warming, the intensity, frequency, and duration of drought events have all increased, posing a significant threat to the agricultural sector. A review study highlights that, since 1970, the land area impacted by drought has expanded, particularly in the Mediterranean, the Southwestern US, Southeast Asia, and Brazil, and the risk of drought is expected to deteriorate (Dai, 2012). Zampieri et al. (2017) quantified the influence of extreme events on wheat yield based on statistics from 1980 to 2010 across multiple countries. They concluded that drought is one of the most significant adverse factors contributing to yield loss. Another study, which focused on Australian wheat yield, also noted that reduced precipitation is the primary reason for the suppression of yield potential in Australian wheat.
 
**The Impact of Excess Precipitation and Timing Sensitivity**
The excess precipitation also harms crops, particularly when it occurs during the critical growth stage. Multiple studies, focusing on different regions, have reached this conclusion (Rosenzweig et al., 2002; Zampieri et al., 2017).  In addition to the negative impact of drought, the study conducted by Zampieri et al. (2017) also illustrates that excess precipitation is another key driver of yield loss. Furthermore, they found that the negative impact exhibits timing sensitivity characteristics; when extreme precipitation events overlap with the crops’ key growth stage, the risk is amplified. Another research points out that, in addition to floods directly caused by excess precipitation, an increased level of soil moisture also harms crops (Rosenzweig et al., 2002). Based on the correction of previous CERES-Maize model deficiencies, this study contradicts the previous study's conclusion, which suggested that increased precipitation has a positive impact on crops. 

#### 2.3.3 The Synergistic Effects of Compound Weather Events
The compound events, whenever they occur simultaneously or in succession, will have a combined impact far greater than the sum of the individual event impacts.

**Concept and Typology**
The fundamental study of compound events set a theoretical framework for Subsequent research (Zscheischler et al., 2020). It is based on a solid literature review proposing clear concepts for four categories of compound events: 1) Preconditioned, 2) multivariate, 3) temporally compounding and 4) spatially compounding. It distinguishes between the concepts of weather and climate, defining weather events as short-term phenomena. However, the climate has two layers: first, it serves as a background that influences the pattern of weather events; second, specific key drivers are already present in climate characteristics.

**Synergistic Yield Reductions & Concurrent Heat and Drought**
Even the discussion about compound events is still limited. Research (Matiu et al., 2017) investigating the interaction effects of drought and heatwave impacts on the yearly change in four crop yields (rice, maize, soybeans, and wheat) has revealed that drought has an amplifying effect on the negative impact of heatwaves. Maize and wheat are more vulnerable to these compound events. Only accessing the impact of each signal event will underestimate the negative influence. 

### 2.4 Transmission Mechanisms of Weather Risk to Economic and Financial Markets
This chapter will discuss the transmission chain from the extreme weather events’ influence to agricultural goods supply site, passing through the commodity futures price to companies' financial performance, which further impacts the stock price and credit market, finally, the physical stock will be amplified or distorted market pricing mechanism and Short-term speculation and lead to unexpected influence on both macro and micro economy which will threaten the global food and nutrient security. 

#### 2.4.1 Direct Impacts on Commodity Markets

**The Classic Case and Mechanism**
A classic study (Roll, 1984) on the impact of weather prediction on the futures orange juice market first reveals the relationship between short-term weather impacts on the market and supply estimation, which will be reflected in futures market price fluctuations. The core findings indicate that unpredictable weather (Frost forecast errors) in the Florida citrus region will be the primary driver of the orange futures price change, which evidences the relationship that exists between the physical world weather and the financial market.

**Global Phenomena and Systematic Impacts**
Further research (Ubilava, 2018) on the impact of global-scale climate phenomena on worldwide commodity prices further enhances the previous conclusion. Based on the 1980-2016 global dataset, this research analyses the relationship between ENSO and global market prices, quantifying the influence of ENSO (a worldwide climate phenomenon) by adopting a TV-STAR (time-varying smooth transition autoregressive) modelling framework. The core finding reveals that El Niño will have a significant impact, driving the global market price of several goods to a higher level, particularly for agricultural products, and this influence is expected to last for several quarters. This research emphasises that the ENSO is an important factor in understanding the market fluctuations and price changes.

#### 2.4.2 Secondary Transmission to Equity and Credit Markets

**Impacts on Corporate Finance**
Under the precondition of climate change, natural disasters and extreme weather events harm related enterprises (such as agricultural companies), affecting their financial performance through increased production costs, decreased income, and reduced asset value, ultimately manifesting as a change in stock price. 
Pagnottoni et al. (2022) employ an event study methodology to examine the impact of natural disasters on the daily change of the global stock market index, revealing that from 2001 to 2019, different disaster events have had varying impacts on global stock markets. Later on, a similar study (Chen et al., 2023) on China's A-share market further revealed the mechanisms behind this phenomenon, the risk exposure to heatwaves has a significantly adverse influence on the corporate performance through increased operation costs and a lower rate of asset return, the China stock market has limitation capability to account for this weather risk.

**Risks in Credit Markets: Short-Term Shocks versus Long-Term Erosion**
Different weather risks threaten the stability of the credit market through two distinct pathways. A recent study employed the Jordá local projections to analyze the credit market's response to disaster shocks over time, concluding that both short-term disaster shocks and long-term extreme weather events negatively impacted the credit market (Nobletz, 2024), while both types of disasters increase corporate default risk, only long-term events like droughts lead to a significant tightening of credit conditions. In contrast, the market does not significantly tighten credit following short-term disasters. Another research provides further evidence of the negative impact on the credit market (Ge et al., 2025). This study revealed that companies located in regions with higher abnormal temperature risks will be charged a higher interest rate for bank loans. This indicates that banks incorporate physical weather risk into their loan pricing mechanism. 

#### 2.4.3 Market Adjustment Mechanisms: Speculation, Policy, and Trade

**Amplification of volatility**
The same weather conditions will have a similar impact on physical agriculture production for crops. However, speculative activities will enhance the movement of commodity prices and lead to a higher level of correlation.  A previous study, conducted by Tang and Xiong (2010) on the U.S. commodity futures market, utilised a panel regression model and the DID-like (difference-in-differences) method. It revealed that the introduction of more investment indices into the market initiates the financialization process and enhances the comovement of commodity futures prices. Moreover, the commodities included in the GSCI & DJ-UBS have an increasing trend with a correlation to oil prices, which leads to a volatility spillover effect, the external market risk, and a single commodity (oil) significant change will transfer to other commodities, hence amplifying the market fluctuation. They conclude that the comovement of market prices is driven by speculation instead of emerging market demand. 

**Buffering of Risks**
Macro-level actions and policies can provide some level of buffering effect against climate and weather risks. A previous study (Liu et al., 2014) developed an integrated model framework to assess the impact of international trade on mitigating food security risks associated with irrigation water shortages in the context of climate change. They concluded that the open and well-operated international market will reallocate the agricultural products from surplus regions to shortage regions, which can significantly buffer the global food security risk. An older study based on an event study methodology reveals a different effect: the announcement of USDA crop reports, which contain new supply-demand information, leads to market price adjustments (Milonas, 1987). 

**Multinational financial institutions influence.**
Several reports published by the European Investment Bank (EIB) suggest that various policies and actions could be adopted to mitigate climate change and extreme weather risks. Firstly, catastrophe bonds and public-private reinsurance arrangements should be utilised for the transfer and sharing of risk, which constitute the key mechanism for stabilising the financial system (European Investment Bank, 2025). Secondly, providing more financial resources to support sustainable transformation and enhance climate resilience should become an important policy direction (European Investment Bank, 2020). Lastly, more practical policies should be implemented, including building climate adaptation capacity and investing in and funding projects to enhance irrigation and infrastructure, which aim to mitigate climate-related risks to the agricultural supply chain (European Investment Bank, 2025).

### 2.5 Chapter Summary and Identification of Research Gaps
The previous research provides a rich resource of models, datasets and various methodologies which constitute solid fundamentals for further analysis, and the discussion focused on both Physical shock and the financial market already illustrates insightful views for climate change and under these preconditions, the extreme weather impact on agriculture production, the economic and financial system. However, the key limitations of previous studies also highlight the direction of further analysis, which focuses on integration, interaction, and explainability. This research will be built upon the methodology and conclusions of prior studies, aiming to overcome their limitations and contribute to filling current research gaps.

**2.5.1 The Methodological Gap: Limitations of Heterogeneous Spatio-temporal Frameworks**
The current research is all utilising datasets based on administrative divisions (such as provinces and states) and Gregorian calendar months. These spatio-temporal units exhibit high levels of climatic and geographical heterogeneity, which enhances noise in model analysis.
This key limitation of the methodology will lead to incomparability in the analysis results between different regions, further compromising model accuracy and explainability, which will result in a weakness in practical policy decisions based on these model analysis results.
This research adopts a data-driven methodology (unsupervised K-means clustering and variance minimisation algorithm) to construct highly homogeneous climate-grain zones and standardised 24 × 15-day periods, which are specific to each country. This improves the data quality and reliability of analysis frameworks at a fundamental level.

**2.5.2 The Interaction & Non-linearity Gap: Insufficiency of Traditional Models**
Although the traditional statistical model (panel regression) can also be used to capture non-linear relationships, the conventional approaches, such as the piecewise linear function, heavily rely on preset assumptions. Consequently, the complexity of the non-linear relationships of the real world is only partially explained by statistical models.
 These key limitations result in a lack of in-depth analysis of the interaction between compound events and necessitate further improvement for a quantitative analysis of the threshold effect using a data-driven methodology rather than subjective assumptions. 
This research will employ an advanced machine learning model to capture the complex, non-linear relationships and interactions between various extreme weather events, thereby improving the threshold quantity methodology. This methodology will be based on the information extracted from raw data to overcome the limitations of existing assumptions.

**2.5.3 The Integration Gap: The Broken Chain between Physical & Financial**
The current analysis exhibits a bipolar distribution, illustrating a lack of interdisciplinary integration; the research either focuses on the impact of climate or weather events on physical agriculture production or only analyses the correlation between those events and the financial market. 
The weak link between these two aspects leads to limitations in understanding the complete picture regarding climate change; the increasing trend of extreme weather events influences mechanisms for both macro-level systematic risk and micro-level physical world individual units' responses. 
This study will focus on this weakness and develop a systematic framework to quantify the transmission effect that links the physical world to yield reduction signals in response to extreme weather events and market pricing mechanisms, which reflect this signal information in market fluctuations.

**2.5.4 The Explainability Gap: The “Black-Box” Challenge**
The machine learning model (XGBoost) has advanced functions, especially in capturing non-linear relationships and providing high-accuracy predictions; however, its “black-box” characteristic limits the explainability of understanding the underlying relationships between features and the target variable.
The strong performance in prediction cannot explain the key drivers of its predicted variable values, which limit the capability of revealing the interaction effect and important influence factors. For example, it cannot answer questions such as how each feature impacts the target variable estimation value and how the correlations between various features contribute to the estimated value.  
This research will develop an explainable machine learning framework (XGBoost + SHAP), aiming to open the “black box” and quantify the contribution of each feature at both global and local levels. It will also evaluate and visualise the interaction effects to significantly improve explainability.
